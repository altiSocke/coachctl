## Paper 1 — Kerksick et al. 2017 ⭐ PRIMARY POSITION STAND

**Title:** International Society of Sports Nutrition Position Stand: Nutrient Timing
**Authors:** Kerksick CM, Arent S, Schoenfeld BJ, Stout JR, Campbell B, Wilborn CD, Taylor L, Kalman D, Smith-Ryan AE, Kreider RB, Willoughby D, Arciero PJ, VanDusseldorp TA, Ormsbee MJ, Wildman R, Greenwood M, Ziegenfuss TN, Aragon AA, Antonio J
**Journal:** Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition
**Year:** 2017 (Aug 29)
**PMID:** 28919842
**PMCID:** PMC5596471
**DOI:** 10.1186/s12970-017-0189-4
**Access:** Free PMC full text
**Type:** Position Stand / Expert Review (ISSN)

**Key findings:**
- Glycogen maximised at 8–12 g/kg/day dietary carbohydrate
- If <4h between sessions: 1.2 g/kg/hr CHO with high-GI sources; adding caffeine (3–8 mg/kg) further accelerates resynthesis
- During exercise >70% VO2max: 30–60 g CHO/hr in 6–8% solution every 10–15 min for efforts >70 min
- Post-exercise (0–2h window): 20–40 g high-quality protein stimulates robust MPS increases
- Protein dose every 3–4 hours (0.25–0.40 g/kg/dose) optimises MPS throughout day
- Casein 30–40 g pre-sleep acutely increases overnight MPS without impairing lipolysis
- Combining CHO + protein (0.8 + 0.2–0.4 g/kg/hr) during recovery reduces total CHO requirement while maintaining glycogen resynthesis

## Paper 2 — Bonilla et al. 2020 ⭐ 4R FRAMEWORK

**Title:** The 4R's Framework of Nutritional Strategies for Post-Exercise Recovery: A Review with Emphasis on New Generation of Carbohydrates
**Authors:** Bonilla DA, Pérez-Idárraga A, Odriozola-Martínez A, Kreider RB
**Journal:** International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health
**Year:** 2020 (Dec 24)
**PMID:** 33375691
**DOI:** 10.3390/ijerph18010103
**Access:** Free PMC
**Type:** Review

**The 4R Framework:**
1. **Rehydrate** — replace fluid and electrolytes; body weight pre vs post to estimate deficit; 1.5 L per kg lost
2. **Refuel** — restore muscle and liver glycogen; 1.0–1.2 g/kg/hr CHO in first 4h if short turnaround; next-day turnaround: 6–10 g/kg/day
3. **Repair** — stimulate muscle protein synthesis; 20–40g high-quality protein within 2h; leucine-rich (whey optimal); 0.25–0.40 g/kg/dose every 3–4h for 24–48h
4. **Rest** — sleep is the master recovery tool; poor sleep blunts MPS and glycogen resynthesis

**New-generation carbohydrates for recovery:**
- Highly-branched cyclic dextrin: faster gastric emptying than maltodextrin, reduced GI distress, may accelerate glycogen resynthesis
- Trehalose: lower glycaemic index than glucose, sustained energy release
- Palatinose (isomaltulose): low GI, promotes fat oxidation — useful for lower-intensity sessions

## Paper 3 — IAAF Consensus Statement 2019 ⭐ ATHLETICS GOLD STANDARD

**Title:** International Association of Athletics Federations Consensus Statement 2019: Nutrition for Athletics
**Journal:** International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism
**Year:** 2019
**PMID:** 30952204
**Type:** Consensus Statement (elite athletics — running primary)

**Key recommendations:**
- Pre-competition meal: 1–4 g/kg carbohydrate, 1–4 hours before, low fibre/fat/protein to reduce GI risk
- During competition (>60 min): 30–60 g CHO/hr; >2.5 hrs: up to 90 g/hr mixed carbs
- Post-competition: protein 20–25g leucine-rich + carbohydrate to initiate recovery
- Daily protein target: 1.4–1.7 g/kg/day for endurance athletes; higher (1.7–2.0) in heavy training
- Fuelling strategies should be practised in training, not debuted in racing
- Specific mention of GI distress as primary limitation in distance running — food format (gels > solids) critical

## Paper 4 — Caravaca et al. 2023 — Ergogenic Effects of Acute CHO During Endurance

**Title:** The ergogenic effects of acute carbohydrate feeding on endurance performance: a systematic review, meta-analysis and meta-regression
**Authors:** Andreu Caravaca L et al.
**Journal:** European Journal of Sport Science
**Year:** 2023
**PMID:** 37449467
**Type:** Systematic Review + Meta-Analysis

**Key findings:**
- Acute CHO feeding during endurance exercise produces significant performance improvement (pooled effect across protocols)
- Greater benefit at durations >90 min
- Mouth-rinsing with CHO solution (even without swallowing) improves performance in short efforts (<60 min) via central/neural mechanisms — no GI ingestion needed
- Dose-response: benefit plateaus around 60 g/hr for single-source; mixed CHO allows further benefit up to ~90 g/hr
- Meta-regression: performance benefit not significantly moderated by training status (recreational and elite both benefit)

## Paper 5 — Bourdas et al. 2021 — Meta-Analysis Carb Solution During Prolonged Exercise

**Title:** Meta-Analysis of Carbohydrate Solution Intake during Prolonged Exercise in Adults: From the Last 45+ Years' Perspective
**Authors:** Bourdas DI, Souglis A, Zacharakis ED, Geladas ND, Travlos AK
**Journal:** Nutrients
**Year:** 2021
**PMID:** 34959776
**Type:** Meta-Analysis (45+ years of literature)

**Key findings:**
- CHO solutions during exercise consistently improve endurance performance vs placebo across 45+ years of research
- Optimal concentration: 6–8% CHO solution
- Effect strongest in efforts lasting 90+ minutes
- Benefits include: delayed glycogen depletion, maintained blood glucose, reduced perception of effort
- Combined electrolyte solution superior to pure CHO solution for efforts >90 min in the heat
- Comparison across decades shows modern mixed-carb formulations (glucose + fructose) outperform older single-source

## Paper 6 — Diaz-Lara et al. 2025 — Low-Carb Availability and Training Adaptations

**Title:** Responses to Exercise with Low Carbohydrate Availability on Muscle Glycogen and Cell Signaling: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis
**Authors:** Diaz-Lara J, Prieto-Bellver G, Guadalupe-Grau A, Bishop DJ
**Journal:** Sports Medicine
**Year:** 2025 (Jan; Epub Oct 2024)
**PMID:** 39352665
**DOI:** 10.1007/s40279-024-02119-9
**Type:** Systematic Review + Meta-Analysis (19 RCTs, Cochrane RoB)

**Key findings — periodised/train-low approach:**
- Low-CHO availability (LOW) upregulates metabolic gene mRNA post-exercise: PDK4 (SMD 1.61), GLUT4 (SMD 1.38), UCP3 (SMD 2.05)
- No significant effect on PGC-1α or TFAM mRNA overall — BUT subgroup with >200 mmol/kg dw glycogen difference DID show ↑ PGC-1α (SMD 2.08, p=0.005)
- Supports "train-low" approach for selected training sessions to enhance mitochondrial signalling
- **Caveat:** LOW reduces training quality, power output, and perceived exertion tolerance — not suitable for quality/intensity sessions
- **Practical:** train-low only in easy/Z2 sessions; compete/quality sessions require full fuelling

## Paper 7 — Gonzalez et al. 2020 — Pre-Exercise Feeding Status and Metabolic Adaptations

**Title:** Impact of pre-exercise feeding status on metabolic adaptations to endurance-type exercise training
**Authors:** Gonzalez JT et al.
**Journal:** Journal of Physiology / Sports Medicine (review)
**Year:** 2020
**PMID:** 33428210
**Type:** Review

**Key findings:**
- Fasted training enhances fat oxidation capacity and mitochondrial signalling compared to fed training
- Performance during fasted sessions is impaired at intensities >70% VO2max
- Recommendation: fasted training appropriate for easy/Z2 sessions only; sessions >75% VO2max require pre-exercise carbohydrate
- Reactive hypoglycaemia risk: carbs ingested 30–75 min before exercise can trigger insulin-mediated glucose drop at exercise onset — mitigate by eating >3h before OR <15 min before exercise

## Paper 8 — Guest et al. 2021 — ISSN Caffeine Position Stand

**Title:** International Society of Sports Nutrition Position Stand: Caffeine and Exercise Performance
**Authors:** Guest NS et al.
**Journal:** Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition
**Year:** 2021
**PMID:** 33388079
**Type:** Position Stand (ISSN)

**Key findings (caffeine-specific, complementing nutrition timing):**
- Effective dose: 3–6 mg/kg body weight, ingested 60 min pre-exercise
- Improves endurance performance, time-trial performance, and maximal strength
- Habitual users: effectiveness reduced; 3–5 day abstinence restores ergogenic effect
- Caffeine gum: faster absorption than capsules (30 min vs 60 min onset) — useful for mid-race top-up
- Does NOT impair hydration at doses ≤6 mg/kg
- Timing in ultra-events: save primary dose for mid-event slump (hours 3–5), secondary dose for hour 7+ or final push

## Intermittent / Team Sports Nutrition — Evidence Summary

*No direct PubMed results for "unihockey/floorball nutrition." Evidence extrapolated from ice hockey, handball, and intermittent exercise literature.*

### What distinguishes intermittent sports nutritionally:

**1. Glycogen demand is high but non-linear**
Repeated sprints (10–15s efforts with 30–90s recovery) are primarily fuelled by phosphocreatine + anaerobic glycolysis. BUT cumulative glycogen depletion over a 60–90 min match is substantial (20–40% of pre-game stores) — comparable to moderate-intensity continuous exercise.

**2. Pre-game nutrition is the primary lever**
Unlike cycling/running where mid-event fuelling is the main challenge, for a 60–90 min unihockey game:
- Mid-game fuelling opportunity is limited (brief halftime only)
- Pre-game carbohydrate status (8–12 g/kg/day in the days before) determines most of glycogen availability
- Pre-game meal (3–4h before): 1–4 g/kg CHO, moderate protein, low fat/fibre

**3. Protein requirements are elevated**
Contact and high-intensity team sports produce more muscle micro-damage than pure endurance cycling. Post-game protein (30–40g leucine-rich) is at the high end of the endurance range.

**4. Hydration in warm indoor environments**
Sweat rate in heated indoor halls can reach 0.8–1.2 L/hr despite cold outdoor temperature. Pre-game hydration strategy (500 mL in 2h before) and post-game rehydration (1.5× fluid loss) are important.

**Sources:** Burke & Hawley (Science, 2018); Jeukendrup (Sports Med, 2017); IAAF Consensus 2019; Jensen et al. (Front Physiol, 2011) on glycogen and insulin sensitivity; general hockey/handball physiology reviews.

---

## Evidence Gaps

1. Direct RCTs on nutrition timing for floorball/unihockey: none indexed on PubMed
2. Optimal CHO dose for intermittent sports <90 min: less studied than continuous endurance
3. Female athlete nutrition timing: systematically underrepresented (mentioned in ISSN Female Athlete Position Stand, PMID 37221858)
4. Long-term effects of train-low periodisation on performance: chronic RCT data limited (Diaz-Lara 2025)
