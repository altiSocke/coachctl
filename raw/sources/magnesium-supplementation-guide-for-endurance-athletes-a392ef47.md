# Magnesium — Supplementation Guide for Endurance Athletes

## Summary

Magnesium is an essential mineral involved in >300 enzymatic reactions, ATP metabolism, muscle contraction, nerve transmission, and blood pressure regulation. Deficiency is common in athletes due to sweat losses and inadequate dietary intake. **Oral supplementation is effective and well-evidenced. Transdermal spray/oil is not.**

---

## Why Athletes Are Often Deficient

- Sweat losses during prolonged exercise deplete Mg
- Western diet is systematically low in Mg (processed food, low leafy greens)
- Hypomagnesaemia defined as serum Mg < 0.75 mmol/L; early signs are non-specific (fatigue, weakness, poor sleep, impaired performance)
- Diuretics and proton pump inhibitors further deplete Mg

---

## Oral Supplementation — What Works

| Form | Bioavailability | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Magnesium glycinate / bisglycinate | High | Best tolerated; low GI side effects; preferred for athletes |
| Magnesium citrate | High | Well-studied; slightly more laxative effect |
| Magnesium chloride | High | Often used in topical products; oral form is effective |
| Magnesium lactate / gluconate | High | Good alternatives |
| Magnesium oxide | Very low | Avoid for raising Mg levels; high GI side effects |
| Magnesium carbonate | Very low | Avoid |

**Dose:** 300–400 mg elemental Mg/day. Split into 2 doses to reduce GI effects. Take at night — supports sleep.

**RDA:** 310–420 mg/day (varies by age/sex). Upper tolerable limit for supplements: 350 mg/day (above this, diarrhoea risk increases).

## Transdermal Magnesium (Spray, Oil, Bath Flakes) — The Evidence

### Verdict: Not supported by evidence

The skin's primary function is barrier protection. The stratum corneum is a lipid matrix that blocks hydrophilic ions. Magnesium in solution is ionised (Mg²⁺) — it cannot passively diffuse through a lipid layer. The hydrated Mg²⁺ ion is ~400× larger than its dehydrated form, making membrane penetration essentially impossible without active transporters — which dead skin cells do not have.

The only potential entry points are hair follicles and sweat glands, which cover **0.1–1% of skin surface** — too small an area to deliver clinically meaningful quantities.

### Studies cited by spray manufacturers — quality assessment

| Study | Problem |
|---|---|
| Shealy (2000) | Unpublished conference abstract; no data; never peer-reviewed |
| Watkins & Josling (2010) | n=9; used hair mineral analysis (not serum Mg); no control group |
| Waring (Epsom Salt Council) | Published only on a commercial website; not peer-reviewed; no control |
| Kass et al. (2017) | n=25 pilot; cream (not spray); serum Mg increase only in non-athletes; not statistically significant in full group |

### Controlled studies showing no absorption

- Israeli army study with Mg-containing skin protectant lotion: **no transdermal absorption** detected in animal models
- Randomised placebo-controlled phase I trial (n=34): **no significant difference** in serum Mg between Mg-lotion and placebo groups after 3 days
- Cadaver skin study: MgCl₂ solution showed similar permeation to phosphate buffer control
- Bath spa immersion study (2h, 35°C): **no change in plasma Mg** concentration

### Conclusion (Gröber et al., *Nutrients* 2017, PMC5579607)

> "Based on the current studies it is extremely alarming if a successful treatment of magnesium fails by propagation of transdermal magnesium, a scientifically not yet proven form of magnesium application."

The relaxing sensation from Mg spray or Epsom salt baths is real — but it comes from warmth, osmotic effects, and placebo, not Mg absorption.

## Magnesium and Muscle Cramps

A widely held belief is that Mg deficiency causes exercise-associated muscle cramps. The evidence is weak:

- Severe Mg deficiency is associated with muscle pain and cramping (correlation, not causation in athletes)
- A **2020 meta-analysis** concluded: Mg supplementation in any form is **unlikely to reduce idiopathic nocturnal or exercise-induced cramps**
- Evidence for pregnancy-associated cramp reduction is conflicting
- Current exercise science points to **neuromuscular fatigue** and **sodium/water losses** as the primary drivers of exercise-associated cramping — not Mg

**Practical implication:** Don't rely on Mg spray pre-race for cramp prevention. Address sodium/hydration strategy instead.

## Evidence-Based Use Cases for Oral Magnesium

| Outcome | Evidence Grade | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Blood pressure reduction | B (Examine) | Dose-dependent; >370 mg/day most effective |
| Sleep quality | C (weak) | Observational data positive; RCTs inconclusive |
| Insulin sensitivity / glucose metabolism | B | Particularly relevant in pre-diabetes |
| Preeclampsia risk reduction | A | Strong evidence in pregnancy |
| Muscle cramps (exercise) | D | Meta-analysis 2020: no significant effect |
| Athletic performance | C | Insufficient evidence for direct ergogenic effect |

---

## Practical Protocol for Endurance Athletes

- **Form:** Magnesium glycinate (best tolerated) or citrate
- **Dose:** 300–400 mg elemental Mg/day
- **Timing:** Evening — supports sleep, avoids GI issues during training
- **Duration:** At least 4–8 weeks to meaningfully raise tissue Mg levels
- **Avoid:** Oxide, carbonate (poor absorption, GI distress)
- **Skip:** Transdermal spray/oil — no evidence of systemic absorption
