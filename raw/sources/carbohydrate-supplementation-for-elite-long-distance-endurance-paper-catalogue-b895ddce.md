## Key Findings — New or Additive vs. Existing Wiki

### Pre-race anxiety and glycogen depletion

Pre-race anxiety activates the sympathetic–adrenal axis and the HPA axis, releasing catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine) and cortisol. Both directly stimulate glycogen phosphorylase.

**Quantified impact:**
- Epinephrine equivalent to pre-race stress over 3h: **>30% muscle glycogen depletion**
- Catecholamine injection over 2h: **41% reduction** in muscle glycogen
- This occurs at rest, before competition begins

**Implication:** An anxious athlete may arrive at the start line with substantially depleted glycogen even after a correct nutrition protocol. The fix is extending the carb-loading window (36–48h) and ensuring morning carb intake even when appetite is suppressed.

---

### Breakfast for afternoon/evening races

Low-carbohydrate breakfast reduces liver glycogen storage, negatively affecting endurance performance **even if lunch is carbohydrate-adequate.** Liver glycogen requires morning CHO intake to restore after overnight fast — the pre-race meal alone does not compensate.

---

### Environmental effects on CHO metabolism

#### Heat
- Optimal marathon WBGT: **7.5°C** — every 1°C above → **~0.1% performance decline**
- At 35°C during 90-min exercise at 55% VO2max:
  - Exogenous CHO oxidation **↓ 10%**
  - Muscle glycogen oxidation **↑ 25%**
- Hot + humid conditions worsen GI distress and compound dehydration/hyponatraemia

#### Cold
- 27% of endurance competitions held in cold (≤10°C)
- Every 1°C below optimal WBGT → **0.3% marathon performance decline**
- Cold at 18°C immersion for 90 min: muscle glycogen + blood glucose **↓ 20%**
- Cold increases resting metabolic rate 2.5–3.5× and endogenous CHO oxidation **6-fold**
- **Hypoglycaemia (<2.5 mmol/L) in cold is a warning sign for hypothermia** (<35°C)
- Warm carbohydrate solutions improve GI absorption in cold and reduce gut discomfort

#### Altitude
- High altitude: endogenous CHO oxidation ↑, exogenous CHO oxidation ↓
- Hypoxia exacerbates both peripheral fatigue (glycogen depletion) and central fatigue (blood glucose fluctuation)
- Appetite suppression common at altitude; athletes struggle to consume even 90g/hr
- 120g/hr shown to postpone fatigue in ultramarathon runners at altitude (single study)
- Athletes native to high altitude respond differently to those from lowland regions

---

### Blood glucose threshold

Blood glucose falling below **3.9 mmol/L** impairs the brain's motor control centres and reduces muscle fibre recruitment capacity. Below **2.5 mmol/L** in cold conditions: risk of hypothermia.

---

### Glycogen supercompensation

- Standard restoration: 8–10 g/kg/day CHO within 24h after glycogen depletion
- Supercompensation: sustained 8 g/kg/day for **36–48h** → muscle glycogen can **double** baseline levels
- Glycogen-to-water ratio: 1g glycogen requires ~2.7g water → small body weight increase during loading is expected and normal
- Final pre-race meal: carbohydrate intake should not exceed **75g** to avoid GI distress

---

### Carbohydrate loading — elite protocol (10-day window)

- **Days 7–10 before race:** natural carb-rich foods, 3 meals/day, no carb supplements; reduce or eliminate carb drinks during non-specialist training
- **Days 1–6 before race:** increase CHO at each meal, resume carb drinks during training, add gel/powder supplementation; increase gradually to avoid GI distress
- **Final meal:** ≤75g CHO; consume ≥2h before race
- **From final meal to start:** small habitual carb solutions to maintain blood glucose; avoid large CHO dose 30–60 min before race; CHO mouth rinse in pre-race holding area
