# External Nasal Dilator Strips in Endurance Sport

*Researched May 2026 | 9 sources incl. 1 meta-analysis | Overall confidence: High*

## Summary

External nasal dilator strips (Breathe Right® and equivalents) mechanically widen the nasal valve and measurably reduce nasal airway resistance at rest. Across 30 years of controlled trials, the consistent finding is that **strips provide no meaningful benefit for VO₂max, power, running economy, or lactate threshold in healthy athletes with normal nasal anatomy.** The only population with credible benefit is athletes with structural or inflammatory nasal obstruction (deviated septum, nasal valve collapse, allergic rhinitis). Sleep quality during congested recovery periods is the best non-performance use case.

---

## Mechanism

The nasal valve (alar cartilage region) is the most common site of dynamic nasal collapse during inspiration. Strips apply outward spring force to widen it, reducing nasal airway resistance by ~20–30% at rest.

Two reasons this doesn't translate to exercise performance:

1. **Exercise-induced nasal decongestion.** Vigorous exercise triggers sympathetic-mediated turbinate vasoconstriction, shrinking the nasal mucosa independently of the strip. Ottaviano et al. (2017) confirmed that pre-exercise PNIF improvement disappears post-exercise.
2. **Oral breathing dominates above ~75% VO₂max.** At race intensity, nasal contribution to tidal volume drops below 20–30%. Widening the nasal passage has negligible impact on total ventilatory flow at that point.

## Evidence Summary

### Healthy athletes — no benefit (Confidence: High)

- **Dinardi et al. 2021** (*Eur Arch Otorhinolaryngol* 278:4045) — systematic review + meta-analysis: ENDS do not significantly improve exercise performance in healthy adult athletes. No effect on VO₂, ventilation, or HR even at 75% VO₂max.
- **Trocchio et al. 1995** (*J Athletic Training* 30:211) — crossover RCT in cyclists: no improvement in anaerobic threshold, VO₂max, or max power.
- **Dinardi et al. 2013** (*Int J Pediatric Otorhinolaryngol* 77:1682) — RCT, 48 adolescent athletes, 1000m race: no improvement in time or cardiorespiratory measures. Adolescents are a best-case population (more likely nasal breathers) — still null.
- **Baker & Behm 1999** (*J Strength Cond Res* 13:242) — light to moderate aerobic exercise: ineffective under all tested conditions.
- **Rydén & Gustavsson 2024** (*Diva-Portal*) — healthy ice hockey players, estimated VO₂max: no significant difference.

### Athletes with nasal obstruction — plausible benefit (Confidence: Moderate)

- **Valsamidis et al. 2024** (*Am J Rhinology & Allergy* 38:92) — athletes with confirmed nasal valve collapse fitted with an internal nasal dilator: significant improvement in VO₂max and aerobic performance (p < 0.001). Mechanism holds when obstruction is the actual bottleneck.

Candidate athletes: deviated septum, structural nasal valve collapse, seasonal allergic rhinitis, exercise-induced rhinitis.

### High intensities specifically (Confidence: High)

Above VT1, all high-intensity studies confirm no effect. Expected physiological result — not a surprising finding. Strips are irrelevant above threshold.

---

## Practical Application

| Situation | Recommendation | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Healthy athlete, normal anatomy, seeking performance gain | Skip — no evidence | High |
| Deviated septum / nasal valve collapse | Worth trialling | Moderate |
| Allergic rhinitis / seasonal congestion | Use during affected periods, especially Z1/Z2 and sleep | Moderate |
| Cold-weather racing (mucosal swelling) | Minor comfort benefit; no performance data | Low |
| Recovery sleep during illness / congestion | Best non-performance use case | Moderate |
| Perceived breathlessness at easy paces | Possible comfort benefit even with unchanged objective metrics | Low |

**Cost:** ~€0.50–1.00/strip. No downside to trialling in obstructed athletes. No reason to use routinely if nasal anatomy is normal.
