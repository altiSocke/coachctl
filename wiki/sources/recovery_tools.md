## SECTION A — FOAM ROLLING (SELF-MYOFASCIAL RELEASE)

---

## Paper 1 — Martínez-Aranda et al. 2024 ⭐ PRIMARY SYSTEMATIC REVIEW

**Title:** Effects of Self-Myofascial Release on Athletes' Physical Performance: A Systematic Review
**Authors:** Martínez-Aranda LM, Sanz-Matesanz M, García-Mantilla ED, González-Fernández FT
**Journal:** Journal of Functional Morphology and Kinesiology
**Year:** 2024 (Jan 11)
**PMID:** 38249097
**PMCID:** PMC10801590
**DOI:** 10.3390/jfmk9010020
**Access:** Free PMC
**Type:** Systematic Review (25 studies, n=517 athletes, databases through Sep 2023)

**Key findings:**
- **Acute positive effects on flexibility and range of motion (ROM):** consistent finding across studies — SMR improves ROM acutely
- **No negative effect on maximal strength and power:** SMR pre-training/warm-up does NOT impair force production (unlike prolonged static stretching)
- **Positive effects on perceptual recovery and DOMS reduction:** athletes subjectively feel better and perceive less soreness
- Some evidence of positive effects on **agility** and **very short high-speed actions** (sprinting)
- Chronic effects on performance: heterogeneous results — no strong consensus for long-term performance gains
- Evidence heterogeneity: protocol parameters (duration, pressure, speed) vary greatly across studies

**Practical takeaway:** Use foam rolling before sessions to improve ROM without strength penalties; use after sessions to reduce DOMS and improve recovery perception. Evidence is strongest for acute use.

## Paper 2 — Laffaye et al. 2019 — FOAM ROLLING AFTER HIIT

**Title:** Self-Myofascial Release Effect With Foam Rolling on Recovery After High-Intensity Interval Training
**Authors:** Laffaye G, Da Silva DT, Delafontaine A
**Journal:** Frontiers in Physiology
**Year:** 2019 (Oct 16)
**PMID:** 31681002
**PMCID:** PMC6805773
**DOI:** 10.3389/fphys.2019.01287
**Access:** Free PMC
**Type:** RCT (20 healthy males, within-subject design — one leg FR, one leg control)

**Key findings:**
- Foam rolling had **no effect on jumping performance** (height, stiffness, power, force)
- FR **reduced DOMS by 50%** in the massaged leg vs. 20% in control leg
- FR increased **hip range of motion by ~4.2%** in massaged vs. unmassaged leg
- HIIT itself caused significant muscle damage: −9.7% in countermovement jump height at 48h
- FR did not restore performance; it reduced soreness

**Practical takeaway:** FR after HIIT doesn't speed up muscular recovery (force/power), but meaningfully reduces perceived soreness and restores ROM. Useful as a comfort and mobility tool post-session — do not expect it to make you physically ready faster.

## Paper 3 — Pagaduan et al. 2022 — CHRONIC FOAM ROLLING

**Title:** Chronic Effects of Foam Rolling on Flexibility and Performance: A Systematic Review of Randomized Controlled Trials
**Authors:** Pagaduan JC, Chang SY, Chang NJ
**Journal:** International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health
**Year:** 2022 (Apr 4)
**PMID:** 35409995
**PMCID:** PMC8998857
**DOI:** 10.3390/ijerph19074315
**Access:** Free PMC
**Type:** Systematic Review of RCTs (9 studies, ≥4 weeks FR intervention)

**Key findings:**
- **Chronic FR for flexibility:** conflicting results — some studies show improvement, others don't
- **Chronic FR for performance:** majority of studies showed **no beneficial effect** on performance measures
- **Chronic FR for recovery:** unclear
- **Conclusion:** Short-term (acute) use of FR is better supported than long-term training programs

**Practical takeaway:** FR is a daily maintenance and pre/post session tool — not a standalone training intervention that builds fitness. Keep it in the warm-up and cool-down, not as a main training emphasis.

## Paper 4 — Scudamore et al. 2021 — FR AFTER DOMS FOR FUNCTIONAL PERFORMANCE

**Title:** Effects of foam rolling for delayed onset muscle soreness on loaded military task performance and perceived recovery
**Authors:** Scudamore EM, Sayer BL, Church JB, Bryant LG, Přibyslavská V
**Journal:** Journal of Exercise Science and Fitness
**Year:** 2021 (Jul)
**PMID:** 33786041
**PMCID:** PMC7970355
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jesf.2021.02.002
**Access:** Free PMC
**Type:** RCT crossover (20 men and women)

**Key findings:**
- FR after DOMS-inducing exercise: **loaded task performance times significantly faster** vs. passive recovery — stair climb (p=0.038), sprint (p=0.011), carry task (p=0.003), shuttle run (p=0.034)
- No significant difference in **perceived soreness** between FR and passive recovery (RMP similar)
- FR improved functional performance without reducing perceived pain

**Key insight:** FR appears to improve neuromuscular readiness for functional/sport-specific tasks despite DOMS, even when subjective soreness score isn't reduced. Mechanism may be improved neuromuscular activation rather than pain reduction.

## Paper 5 — Arbiza et al. 2024 — FR vs. ACTIVE vs. PASSIVE RECOVERY

**Title:** Effect of foam rolling recovery on pain and physical capacity after resistance exercises: A randomized crossover trial
**Authors:** Arbiza BCC et al.
**Journal:** Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies
**Year:** 2024 (Jan)
**PMID:** 38432810
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jbmt.2023.11.022
**Type:** RCT crossover (37 physically healthy men; 3 conditions: passive, active, foam rolling)

**Key findings:**
- FR and active recovery both improved post-exercise **strength** by ~5.2% vs. passive recovery at 1h
- FR and active recovery both improved **agility by ~4%** vs. baseline
- **Only FR reduced pain** at 24h (22.8%), 48h (39.2%), and 72h (59.7%) vs. passive recovery
- Active recovery (light cycling) did not reduce pain at any timepoint; FR was uniquely effective for DOMS management

**Practical takeaway:** After strength/resistance sessions, FR is the single best short recovery intervention: restores strength and agility faster than passive rest, AND reduces 24–72h DOMS — an advantage active recovery does not share.

## Paper 6 — Kaya et al. 2021 — FR DURING HALF-TIME IN TEAM SPORTS

**Title:** Foam rolling during a simulated half-time attenuates subsequent soccer-specific performance decrements
**Authors:** Kaya S, Cug M, Behm DG
**Journal:** Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies
**Year:** 2021 (Apr)
**PMID:** 33992244
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jbmt.2020.12.009
**Type:** Acute controlled crossover (13 male soccer players)

**Key findings:**
- FR during simulated half-time (5 exercises × 45s × both legs): **sprint speed maintained** in FR condition; passive half-time produced significant sprint speed decline (all 3 sprint measures)
- Only a 2.1% decrease in first 5 sprints with FR (vs. significant decline in passive condition)
- No difference in pass accuracy, blood lactate, HR, or total quality of recovery scores
- FR at half-time is a **practical intervention for team sport athletes** to preserve sprint performance in second half

**Unihockey application:** During the 10-minute break between unihockey periods, 2–3 minutes of targeted foam rolling (quads, hip flexors, calves) may preserve explosive sprint performance in the second period.

## Paper 7 — Kerautret et al. 2021 — MANUAL MASSAGE VS. FOAM ROLLING REVIEW

**Title:** Selective Effects of Manual Massage and Foam Rolling on Perceived Recovery and Performance: Current Knowledge and Future Directions
**Authors:** Kerautret Y, Di Rienzo F, Eyssautier C, Guillot A
**Journal:** Frontiers in Physiology
**Year:** 2021 (Dec 21)
**PMID:** 33408640
**PMCID:** PMC7779631
**DOI:** 10.3389/fphys.2020.598898
**Access:** Free PMC
**Type:** Integrative Review

**Key findings:**
- Both manual massage and foam rolling improve **perceived recovery** and **ROM**
- Manual massage: passive, retroactive motor control (physiotherapist applies force); slightly stronger evidence for deep tissue effects
- Foam rolling: active, proactive motor control (self-directed); practical advantage = accessible anywhere, no therapist needed
- FR adds a neural/proprioceptive component absent from passive massage
- Neither technique has strong evidence for improving force production or aerobic capacity

## Paper 8 — Brophy-Williams et al. 2019 ⭐ COMPRESSION SOCKS — SUBSEQUENT PERFORMANCE

**Title:** Wearing compression socks during exercise aids subsequent performance
**Authors:** Brophy-Williams N, Driller MW, Kitic CM, Fell JW, Halson SL
**Journal:** Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport
**Year:** 2019 (Jan)
**PMID:** 30554612
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jsams.2018.06.010
**Type:** Counterbalanced crossover (12 male runners, 2 × 5km time trials with 1h recovery)

**Key findings:**
- Wearing compression socks during TT1: **attenuated performance decline** in TT2 — decline in CON was significantly greater than COMP (9.6s, d=0.67, p<0.01)
- No significant difference in VO₂, blood lactate, or calf volume between conditions
- Perceptual measures (soreness, fatigue, recovery) similar — effect appears physiological, not purely psychometric
- Mechanism unclear: improved venous return / oxygen delivery, reduced muscle oscillation, or better running mechanics

**Practical:** Wearing compression socks during a hard session or race may preserve performance in a subsequent session within the same day (back-to-back training days, stage races, double training days).

## Paper 9 — Zadow et al. 2022 — COMPRESSION SOCKS REDUCE GI DAMAGE

**Title:** Compression Socks Reduce Running-Induced Intestinal Damage
**Authors:** Zadow EK et al.
**Journal:** Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research
**Year:** 2022 (Sep 1); Epub 2020 Oct 15
**PMID:** 33065702
**DOI:** 10.1519/JSC.0000000000003870
**Type:** RCT (46 marathon runners; compression vs. no compression)

**Key findings:**
- I-FABP (intestinal fatty acid-binding protein, marker of gut epithelial damage): increase after marathon was **107%** in control vs. **38%** in compression sock group (p=0.046, d=0.59)
- Mechanism: compression socks may improve **splanchnic (gut) blood flow** by enhancing central venous return, partially counteracting the blood-flow redistribution to muscles during exercise
- Relevant to GI distress management in long endurance events

**Practical:** Compression socks may reduce exercise-induced GI syndrome during marathons and long cycling events — an underappreciated benefit beyond muscle recovery.

## Paper 10 — Kabongo et al. 2022 — UPPER LEG COMPRESSION IN MARATHONS

**Title:** Do upper leg compression garments aid performance and reduce exercise-induced muscle damage in recreational marathon runners?
**Authors:** Kabongo KM, Emeran A, Bosch AN
**Journal:** South African Journal of Sports Medicine
**Year:** 2022
**PMID:** 36815926
**PMCID:** PMC9924514
**Type:** Controlled trial (18 runners; CG group n=10, control n=8)

**Key findings:**
- Upper leg CGs reduced **post-race VAS pain** for hamstring (p=0.04), knee flexion (p=0.02), hip extension (p=0.04) immediately post-race and 2 days post-race
- No significant difference in **race performance**, mid-thigh swelling, or Likert soreness scores
- Benefit may be partly psychological (no placebo control)

## Paper 11 — Carvalho et al. 2021 — 24h COMPRESSION POST-EXERCISE

**Title:** Effects of 24h Compression Interventions with Different Garments on Recovery Markers during Running
**Authors:** Carvalho J et al.
**Journal:** Life (Basel)
**Year:** 2021 (Aug 30)
**PMID:** 34575054
**PMCID:** PMC8471437
**DOI:** 10.3390/life11090905
**Access:** Free PMC
**Type:** Crossover RCT (10 trained runners, 4 garment conditions)

**Key findings:**
- 24h post-exercise compression stockings (including menthol/camphor versions) did **not significantly affect** running kinematics, skin temperature, HR, comfort, or recovery perception the following day
- Suggests wearing compression for recovery overnight may be **less effective** than wearing during or immediately after exercise

## Evidence Summary

### Foam Rolling

| Use case | Evidence strength | Practical recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-session ROM improvement | Strong | 60–90s per muscle group; replaces or complements dynamic warm-up |
| Pre-session — does it hurt strength/power? | Strong (no harm) | Safe to use pre-session without power penalty |
| Post-session DOMS reduction | Moderate-Strong | 5–10 min targeted rolling; reduces 24–72h soreness |
| Functional performance after DOMS | Moderate | FR helps maintain task performance even when sore |
| Half-time / mid-game use (team sports) | Preliminary | 2–3 min targeted rolling preserves sprint speed in second period |
| Long-term training adaptation | Weak / unclear | Don't replace training with foam rolling |

### Compression Garments

| Use case | Evidence strength | Practical recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| Wearing during exercise — subsequent performance | Moderate | Wear during hard sessions/races if doing back-to-back days |
| Wearing during marathon — GI protection | Moderate | Compression socks reduce gut epithelial damage by ~65% |
| Post-exercise pain reduction | Weak-Moderate | Upper leg compression reduces subjective soreness |
| 24h overnight recovery | Weak | Limited benefit from overnight compression stockings |

---

## Evidence Gaps

1. Optimal foam roller hardness (firm vs. soft) — limited comparative RCT data
2. Vibration foam rolling (e.g., Blackroll Vibe, Theragun) vs. standard rolling — emerging evidence, not yet in meta-analyses
3. Compression garment pressure levels — most studies use commercial garments without specifying mmHg; optimal pressure unknown
4. Female athlete-specific data for both foam rolling and compression: underrepresented
5. Direct comparison of foam rolling vs. manual massage for DOMS — no head-to-head RCT
