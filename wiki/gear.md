## Running Shoes

### Do shoes prevent injuries?

**Short answer: No shoe type convincingly reduces running injury risk in RCTs.**

The Cochrane review (Relph 2022, 12 RCTs, n=11,240) found:
- Neutral/cushioned vs. minimalist: no difference in injury rates (low certainty)
- Soft vs. hard midsole: no difference (low certainty)
- **Foot posture prescription (arch type):** does NOT reduce injuries — moderate-certainty evidence; prescribing motion-control shoes to overpronators is not evidence-based

**Practical implication:** Fit, comfort, and personal preference are as defensible as any biomechanical prescription. Running in shoes that feel comfortable to *you* is the most evidence-consistent recommendation.

### Shoe construction and biomechanics

| Feature | Effect | Practical guidance |
|---|---|---|
| **Soft midsole** | ↓ impact forces and loading rates | Good for high-volume easy training; reduces acute loading |
| **Hard/firm midsole** | ↑ plantar sensory feedback; ↑ energy return | Better for speed work where proprioception matters |
| **Thick midsole** | More cushioning, ↑ shock attenuation | May ↓ ground feel; relevant for ultra distances |
| **High stiffness (carbon plate)** | ↑ running economy ~3–4% (curved plate only) | Race day / quality sessions — not daily training |
| **Minimalist / low-drop shoe** | ↑ Achilles tendon strength; ↑ forefoot load | Gradual transition required; ↑ metatarsal stress |
| **Heel-toe drop** | Insufficient RCT evidence for injury guidelines | Personal preference; 4–8mm is most common |

### Carbon plate shoes (Vaporfly / Alphafly / Next%)

**The 4% benefit is real — but nuanced:**
- Meta-analysis (Rodrigo-Carranza 2022, 12 studies): **~3.45% RE improvement** with curved carbon plate + foam vs. no plate; flat plates show no significant benefit
- Performance benefit via **↑ stride length** and **↑ contact time** — not by altering muscle mechanics (Beck 2020)
- Benefit is **speed-dependent**: larger gains at faster running speeds; smaller at easy pace
- On **hilly terrain** (Whiting 2022): savings reduce to ~2.8% uphill and ~2.7% downhill vs. ~3.8% on flat

**⚠️ Injury risk — navicular stress fractures:**
- Tenforde et al. 2023: 5 navicular bone stress injury cases linked to carbon plate shoe use
- Mechanism: altered foot/ankle biomechanics increase load on midfoot structures
- Risk management:
  - **Transition gradually**: ≥4 weeks from conventional to carbon plate shoes
  - **Not for daily training**: reserve for race day, time trials, or key quality sessions
  - **Volume limit**: ≤30% of weekly mileage in carbon plate shoes during transition
  - Runners with prior midfoot/navicular stress injuries: extra caution

### Minimalist / low-drop shoes

- Can improve running economy by strengthening intrinsic foot muscles and Achilles tendon
- **Transition is essential**: switch too fast → metatarsal stress fractures (↑ forefoot loading)
- Protocol: reduce weekly volume in new shoes by 50%; add 10% per week; full transition in 8–12 weeks
- Not recommended for runners with Achilles tendinopathy history during flare-ups

### Practical shoe rotation

| Role | Characteristics | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Daily trainer | Moderate cushion, 8–10mm drop, durable | Brooks Ghost, Nike Pegasus, ASICS Cumulus |
| Long run | Maximum cushion, 8–10mm drop | Hoka Clifton, Brooks Glycerin, ASICS Nimbus |
| Speed / race | Carbon plate + ZoomX-type foam, curved plate | Nike Vaporfly/Alphafly, Adidas Adizero Pro, Puma Deviate |
| Trail / mountain | Aggressive lug, low or zero drop, rock plate | Salomon Speedcross, Hoka Speedgoat |

**Do not race in carbon plate shoes for the first time on race day.** Use them in at least 2–3 quality training sessions first.

## Foam Rolling (Blackroll)

### Mechanism

Foam rolling (self-myofascial release, SMR) applies compressive load to soft tissue via a cylinder. Proposed mechanisms:
- **Thixotropic effect:** repeated compression reduces fascia viscosity → improved mobility
- **Neurological:** sustained pressure stimulates Golgi tendon organs → reduces muscle spindle activity → temporary tone reduction
- **Vasodilation:** mechanical stimulus increases local blood flow → faster metabolite clearance
- **Gate control:** proprioceptive input competes with pain signals → reduced DOMS perception

### Evidence summary

| Outcome | Evidence | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Acute ROM improvement | Strong | 60–90s/muscle group improves ROM without strength penalty |
| DOMS reduction | Moderate-Strong | FR reduces subjective soreness 24–72h post-session by ~40–60% vs. passive rest |
| Strength/power before session | Strong (no harm) | Does NOT impair maximal force or power — safe pre-session |
| Functional performance during DOMS | Moderate | Maintains loaded task performance even when sore |
| Half-time sprint preservation (team sports) | Preliminary | ~2–3 min rolling at half-time attenuates second-half sprint decline |
| Chronic training adaptation | Weak | No evidence for long-term fitness gains from rolling alone |

### Protocols

**Pre-session warm-up (5–8 min):**
- 60 seconds per muscle group
- Target: calves, quads, IT band, hip flexors, thoracic spine
- Moderate pressure: uncomfortable but not sharp pain
- Follow with dynamic movement (leg swings, hip circles)

**Post-session cool-down (8–12 min):**
- 60–90 seconds per muscle group
- Target: worked muscles of the session (e.g., post-run: calves, quads, hamstrings, glutes)
- Pause on tender spots for 5–10 seconds then continue
- Perform within 30–60 min of exercise while tissue is still warm

**After a hard session / next-day DOMS:**
- 10–15 min full lower body (calves, peroneals, achilles/soleus, quads, IT band, TFL, glutes, hamstrings)
- Helps restore neuromuscular readiness for next session even when soreness persists
- Add hip flexor + thoracic spine for cyclists

**Half-time / between periods (team sports — unihockey):**
- 2–3 min targeted: quads (1 min) + hip flexors (30s each side) + calves (30s each side)
- Preserves sprint performance in second period (Kaya 2021)

### Key areas by sport

| Sport | Priority areas | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Running** | Calves/soleus, IT band, glutes, hip flexors | High cumulative stress; hip flexor tightness = stride restriction |
| **Cycling** | Hip flexors, quads, thoracic spine, piriformis | Sustained hip flexion; aero position = thoracic load |
| **Unihockey** | Quads, hip flexors, calves, adductors | Sprint/deceleration load; change of direction stress |

### Blackroll product selection

| Roller type | Hardness | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Standard / Medium | Medium | Most athletes; all-purpose |
| Hard / Black | Firm | Athletes with high tissue density, deeper pressure needed |
| Soft / Orange | Gentle | Beginners, acute soreness, sensitive areas |
| Short roller | Any | Travel, targeted work (calf, shin) |
| Ball (Blackroll Ball) | Hard | Point-specific: glutes (piriformis), feet, thoracic facets |
| Vibrating (Blackroll Vibe) | Variable | Enhanced neurological effect; emerging evidence of faster ROM gains |

**Avoid direct rolling on joints (knee, ankle), spine, and acute injuries.** Roll along the muscle belly.

## Compression Garments

### During exercise

Wearing compression socks or calf sleeves during hard runs:
- **Preserves subsequent performance** in back-to-back sessions (Brophy-Williams 2019): athletes who wore compression socks during session 1 declined less in session 2 (9.6s less drop-off in 5km)
- **Reduces gut epithelial damage** in marathon running by ~65% (Zadow 2022): compression socks improve venous return → partial preservation of splanchnic blood flow → less intestinal damage
- Mechanism unclear but likely: improved venous return, reduced calf muscle oscillation, enhanced oxygen delivery

### After exercise

- **Upper leg compression** reduces subjective pain at 24–48h post-marathon (Kabongo 2022)
- **Overnight compression (24h):** limited benefit in the research; don't rely on it as a primary recovery tool (Carvalho 2021)
- Compression garments are not consistently superior to active recovery (easy cycling/walking) for objective performance markers

### Practical recommendations

| When | What | Why |
|---|---|---|
| During long run / race | Compression socks (calf-length) | Preserve GI integrity; may help subsequent-day performance |
| During hard intervals | Calf sleeves or socks | Reduce muscle oscillation, possible performance maintenance |
| Immediately post-run (1–2h) | Compression tights or calf sleeves | Pain reduction, enhanced venous return |
| Recovery overnight | Optional | Limited evidence — comfort rather than physiology |

**Contraindications:** Peripheral arterial disease, deep vein thrombosis. If feet feel numb or cold, compression is too tight.

## Insoles and Orthotics

> Full evidence detail in `wiki/general/foot_health.md`. Summary here.

Meta-analysis of 12 RCTs (Pires Neves 2022, n=5,321): **foot orthoses reduce lower limb injury risk by 40%** (RR=0.60, moderate evidence). This is stronger evidence than was previously assumed — the shoe-type Cochrane (Relph 2022) found no benefit from shoe *category*, but insoles as an added intervention do work.

**Custom vs. off-the-shelf:**
- Custom FOs: better comfort and pressure relief, but **slightly worsen running economy** (SMD −0.25) — relevant for performance runners
- Off-the-shelf arch-support (e.g. Superfeet, Sidas, Redi-Thotics): comparable pressure reduction, neutral economy effect, fraction of the cost
- **For most athletes: OTS first.** Custom only if OTS insufficient after 6–8 weeks or specific structural diagnosis

**Use insoles when:**
- Symptomatic: plantar fasciitis, MTSS, patellofemoral pain → insoles are part of first-line treatment
- Recurring lower limb injuries → preventive OTS insole justified by moderate evidence
- High training volume (>50 km/week) → consider preventive use

**Foot strengthening is the long-term solution** — insoles support a weak arch; exercises build it. See `foot_health.md` for IFM protocol and minimalist walking shortcut.

## Quick Decision Guide

```
"What shoes should I wear today?"
│
├── Easy/recovery run → daily trainer (max cushion, comfortable)
├── Long run → long-run shoe (max cushion, durable)
├── Intervals/tempo → speed trainer or racing flat (lighter, moderate stiffness)
├── Race day → carbon plate (if adapted; 4+ weeks transition complete)
└── Trail run → trail shoe with appropriate lug depth

"Should I foam roll?"
│
├── Before session → YES, 5–8 min, improves ROM, no strength penalty
├── After session → YES, 8–12 min, reduces DOMS
├── DOMS the next day → YES, helps functional readiness
├── At halftime (unihockey) → YES, 2–3 min targeted
└── As a substitute for training → NO, no fitness adaptation benefit

"Should I wear compression?"
│
├── Long run / marathon → YES, compression socks, reduces GI damage
├── Double training day → YES, wear during session 1
├── Post-race recovery → YES, wear 1–2h post-race
└── Overnight → OPTIONAL, minimal evidence
```
