## Paper 1 — Warneke et al. 2025 ⭐ PRIMARY META-ANALYSIS

**Title:** Acute and Chronic Effects of Stretching on Running Economy: A Systematic Review with Meta-Analysis  
**Authors:** Warneke K, Zechner M, Siegel SD, Jochum D, Brunssen L, Konrad A  
**Journal:** Sports Medicine Open  
**Year:** 2025 (published May 30)  
**PMID:** 40442558  
**PMCID:** PMC12122984  
**DOI:** 10.1186/s40798-025-00859-0  
**Access:** Free PMC full text  
**Type:** Systematic Review + Meta-Analysis (PRISMA 2020, PICOS, robust variance estimation)  
**Quality:** PEDro score 4.88 (fair); GRADE: low certainty of evidence  

**Key findings:**
- 15 acute studies + 2 chronic studies, n=181 total participants
- **No significant effect of any stretching type (SS, DS, PNF) on running economy acutely** (p=0.21–0.65)
- The old belief that stretching reduces tendon stiffness → impairs RE is NOT supported at normal warm-up durations
- Prolonged/extreme stretching (>60s per muscle group) remains theoretically problematic for stiffness-dependent RE — not tested at typical warm-up durations
- Chronic effects: only 2 studies found — insufficient for meta-analysis; large evidence gap
- **Conclusion:** it is unreasonable to avoid pre-running stretching to prevent RE decreases

**Methodological limits:** small sample sizes; heterogeneous protocols; no mechanistic measurement of stiffness; RE tested at varying velocities (65–90% VO2max).

## Paper 2 — Panascì et al. 2024 ⭐ KEY RCT

**Title:** Moderate-Duration Dynamic Stretching During Warm-up Improves Running Economy and Running Performance in Recreational Distance Runners  
**Authors:** Panascì M, Ferrando V, Bisio A, Filipas L, Di Gennaro S, Puce L, Ruggeri P, Faelli E  
**Journal:** International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance  
**Year:** 2024 (published Nov 28; print 2025 Jan 1)  
**PMID:** 39608347  
**DOI:** 10.1123/ijspp.2023-0468  
**Type:** Randomized Controlled Trial (crossover design)  
**n:** 12 recreational male runners  

**Protocol:**
- Three conditions randomised: NS (no stretch), SS (60s/leg static), DS (60s/leg dynamic)
- SS/DS conditions: 5 min run + 10 min stretching
- NS condition: 15 min run only
- Tests: submaximal continuous run at 75% and 85% VT2 + time-to-exhaustion at vVO2max

**Key findings:**
- **DS significantly improved RE at 75%VT2 vs NS (p<0.001) and vs SS (p<0.05)**
- Time to exhaustion and total distance improved with DS vs NS (p<0.001) and vs SS (p<0.01)
- SS at 60s/leg: no significant improvement in RE or performance (neutral, not harmful)
- No differences in RPE across conditions
- **Critical dose insight:** 60s/leg of DS is the "moderate duration" threshold that produces benefit

## Paper 3 — Faelli et al. 2021

**Title:** The Effect of Static and Dynamic Stretching during Warm-Up on Running Economy and Perception of Effort in Recreational Endurance Runners  
**Authors:** Faelli E, Panascì M, Ferrando V, Bisio A, Filipas L, Ruggeri P, Bove M  
**Journal:** International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health  
**Year:** 2021 (Aug 8)  
**PMID:** 34444136  
**PMCID:** PMC8391672  
**DOI:** 10.3390/ijerph18168386  
**Access:** Free PMC full text  
**Type:** Randomized crossover counterbalanced study  
**n:** 8 male recreational endurance runners (age 36±11 years)  

**Protocol:**
- Three conditions: SS (10min run + 5min SS), DS (10min run + 5min DS), NS (15min run only)
- SS: static holds to point of discomfort (POD)
- DS: dynamic movements (unspecified duration, shorter than Paper 2)
- Test: running-to-exhaustion

**Key findings:**
- **Both SS (p<0.05) and DS (p<0.01) significantly improved RE vs NS**
- RPE was lower in SS (p<0.05) and DS (p<0.01) vs NS
- No differences in physiological/metabolic responses across conditions
- Note: shorter SS duration than Paper 2 (5 min total vs 10 min total) — both were beneficial
- **Authors encourage recreational runners to include stretching in warm-up**

**Contrast with Paper 2:** shorter duration SS (5 min vs 10 min) still showed benefit here, while 10-min SS in Paper 2 did not. Likely explained by differences in per-muscle hold duration vs total time.

## Paper 4 — Ullman, Fernandez & Klein 2021

**Title:** Effects of Isometric Exercises versus Static Stretching in Warm-up Regimens for Running Sport Athletes: A Systematic Review  
**Authors:** Ullman ZJ, Fernandez MB, Klein M  
**Journal:** International Journal of Exercise Science  
**Year:** 2021 (Oct 1)  
**PMID:** 35096248  
**PMCID:** PMC8734046  
**DOI:** 10.70252/FDTW6296  
**Access:** Free PMC full text  
**Type:** Systematic Review  

**Key findings:**
- Isometric warm-up exercises (e.g. wall sits, isometric quads, calf holds) **preserve force output better** than static stretching pre-exercise
- SS reduces short-term maximal force production (relevant for sprint/power athletes, less relevant for endurance)
- **For pure endurance events at submaximal effort:** difference between IS and SS is minimal
- **Recommendation:** isometric warm-up preferred if explosive power is required immediately after warm-up (relevant for unihockey, hockey game starts)

## Paper 5 — Kurt, Gürol & Nebioğlu 2023

**Title:** Effects of Traditional Stretching versus Self-Myofascial Release Warm-up on Physical Performance in Well-Trained Female Athletes  
**Authors:** Kurt C, Gürol B, Nebioğlu İÖ  
**Journal:** Journal of Musculoskeletal and Neuronal Interactions  
**Year:** 2023 (Mar 1)  
**PMID:** 36856101  
**Access:** Free PMC full text  
**Type:** RCT (3 interventions, randomised, 72hr washout)  
**Population:** well-trained female athletes  

**Key findings:**
- **Dynamic stretching significantly improved jump height and hamstring flexibility** vs static stretching and SMR (foam rolling)
- SMR (foam rolling) + DS outperformed traditional SS alone for athletic performance
- For jump performance (relevant to team sports / unihockey): DS > SS pre-activity
- Foam rolling does not replace stretching but complements it for ROM and performance

## Paper 6 — Parr et al. 2025

**Title:** Injury Epidemiology and Preventative Strategies in Triathletes: A Study of the 2022 Season  
**Authors:** Parr JJ, Twum F, Dobson SR, Ziegler HM, Hayes T  
**Journal:** Journal of Sport Rehabilitation  
**Year:** 2025 (Apr 12; print 2026 Jan 1)  
**PMID:** 40216382  
**DOI:** 10.1123/jsr.2023-0320  
**Type:** Epidemiological survey (retrospective)  
**n:** 219 triathletes who reported ≥1 injury  

**Key findings:**
- 58.5% of injuries classified as overuse; 73.8% lower-extremity
- 87% had to miss or modify training
- **Among injury-free triathletes: 72.2% credited strength training; 64.9% credited stretching/foam rolling**
- Stretching + foam rolling cited as second most important injury prevention strategy after strength training
- Specific to endurance multi-sport athletes (running + cycling combination)

## Paper 7 — Park et al. 2018 (Ice Hockey context)

**Title:** The Effect of Warm-ups with Stretching on the Isokinetic Moments of Collegiate Men  
**Authors:** Park HK, Jung MK, Park E, Lee CY (Anyang Halla Ice Hockey Club), Jee YS, Eun D, Cha JY, Yoo J  
**Journal:** Journal of Exercise Rehabilitation  
**Year:** 2018 (Feb 26)  
**PMID:** 29511656  
**PMCID:** PMC5833972  
**DOI:** 10.12965/jer.1835210.605  
**Access:** Free PMC full text  
**Type:** Controlled trial  
**n:** 13 physically active male collegiate students + ice hockey club athletes  

**Key findings:**
- Aerobic warm-up + stretching (ASW) vs warm-up only vs no warm-up
- **No significant differences in knee flexor/extensor peak torque or total work** across conditions (SS at typical warm-up duration neutral on force output)
- **Psychological readiness significantly higher in ASW group** — athletes felt more confident and prepared
- Short SS during warm-up does not impair muscle strength/power in this population
- Implication for hockey/unihockey: the confidence/psychological priming effect of stretching may be as important as physiological effects

## Stop-and-Go / Indoor Team Sports: Extrapolated Evidence

*No direct RCTs on unihockey/floorball stretching were found. Evidence extrapolated from:*

### Handball & Soccer Literature (well-studied analogues)

**FIFA 11+ Programme (Soligard et al., BJSM):**
- Dynamic warm-up protocol reduces lower limb injury risk ~30–50% in team sports
- Includes running drills, dynamic stretching, strength, balance — not static stretching
- Most applicable model for unihockey pre-game warm-up

**Adductor/Groin Injury Prevention:**
- Copenhagen Adductor Programme (eccentric) > static adductor stretching for injury prevention
- PNF adductor stretching improves ROM more than static — relevant for skating-stride equivalent in unihockey running movements

**Key injuries in stop-and-go indoor sports (from handball/soccer/hockey literature):**
- Hamstring strain — explosive sprint from static → dynamic stretching + eccentric (Nordic curls)
- Adductor/groin — lateral cutting, sudden direction change → Copenhagen programme
- Ankle sprains — dorsiflexion restriction → calf stretching + proprioception training
- Patellar tendinopathy — jump-landing load → eccentric protocol + quad flexibility

## Evidence Gaps / Future Research

1. No long-term RCTs on chronic stretching effects on running economy (Warneke 2025 identified this explicitly)
2. No cycling-specific stretching RCTs on performance or injury prevention
3. No floorball/unihockey-specific stretching literature indexed on PubMed
4. Mechanistic studies on stiffness changes from typical warm-up stretch durations are lacking
5. Female endurance athletes underrepresented in stretching RCTs
