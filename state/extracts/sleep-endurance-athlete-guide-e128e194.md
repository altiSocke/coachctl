# Sleep — Endurance Athlete Guide

*General sleep science for endurance athletes. Personal sleep logs and individual protocols live in the personal wiki.*

---

## Why endurance athletes sleep differently on rest days

Endurance athletes commonly report worse sleep quality on rest days than on training days. The mechanism is well understood:

- **Adenosine buildup** — physical activity accelerates adenosine accumulation in the brain. Adenosine is the primary sleep-pressure molecule; higher levels = faster sleep onset and deeper slow-wave sleep. On rest days, adenosine accumulates more slowly → less sleep pressure at bedtime.
- **Core body temperature** — exercise raises core temp; the subsequent drop triggers sleep onset. On rest days, the temperature curve is flatter and the drop is smaller, delaying sleep onset.
- **Cortisol rhythm** — moderate-intensity training suppresses evening cortisol. On complete rest days, cortisol may remain slightly elevated in the evening, increasing alertness.
- **Psychological arousal** — athletes with high training loads are habituated to physical fatigue as the cue for sleep. Absence of that fatigue can cause restlessness and racing thoughts.

**Key reference:** Halson SL (2014). *Sleep in elite athletes and nutritional interventions to enhance sleep.* Sports Medicine, 44(S1), 13–23.

## Caffeine clearance on rest days

Caffeine half-life is ~5–6 hours in most adults, but exercise accelerates hepatic metabolism. On training days, caffeine consumed at 14:00 may be largely cleared by 20:00. On rest days, the same dose persists longer.

**Practical rule:** move caffeine cutoff 1–2 hours earlier on rest days. If your training-day cutoff is 15:00, use 13:00 on rest days.

**Key reference:** Arnaud MJ (1987). *The pharmacology of caffeine.* Progress in Drug Research, 31, 273–313. Also: Doherty M & Smith PM (2005). *Effects of caffeine ingestion on exercise testing.* International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism, 15(4), 423–439.

## Sleep architecture and recovery

Sleep is not uniform — it cycles through stages with distinct recovery functions:

| Stage | Function | Disrupted by |
|---|---|---|
| **Slow-wave sleep (SWS / N3)** | Physical repair, GH release, glycogen restoration | Alcohol, high core temp, late training |
| **REM sleep** | Motor learning, skill consolidation, emotional regulation | Caffeine, sleep debt, alcohol |
| **Light sleep (N1/N2)** | Transition, memory consolidation | Noise, light, irregular schedule |

Endurance athletes benefit most from maximising SWS (physical repair) and REM (motor pattern consolidation). Both are disrupted by alcohol and late-evening high-intensity training.

**Key reference:** Dattilo M et al. (2011). *Sleep and muscle recovery: endocrinological and molecular basis for a new and promising hypothesis.* Medical Hypotheses, 77(2), 220–222.

## Practical sleep hygiene for endurance athletes

### Rest day protocol

| Issue | Fix |
|---|---|
| Low adenosine / low sleep pressure | 20–30min evening walk — enough to build mild fatigue without adding training load |
| Elevated core temp | Bedroom 17–19°C. Cool shower 60–90min before bed accelerates temp drop |
| Caffeine lingering | Cutoff 13:00 on rest days (vs 15:00 on training days) |
| Psychological restlessness | Journaling or structured wind-down routine (reading, breathing) — externalise the mental load |
| Irregular schedule | Keep the same wake time regardless of sleep quality. Sleep debt resolves faster than circadian disruption |

### General protocol (all days)

- **Consistent wake time** — the single most evidence-backed sleep intervention. Anchor the circadian clock.
- **Dark, cool room** — blackout curtains + 17–19°C. Light is the strongest circadian disruptor.
- **No screens 45min before bed** — blue light suppresses melatonin onset by ~90min (Gooley et al. 2011).
- **Avoid alcohol** — even moderate doses fragment SWS and suppress REM in the second half of the night.
- **Don't lie in bed awake >20min** — get up, do something calm in dim light, return when sleepy. Lying awake trains the brain to associate bed with wakefulness (stimulus control therapy).

### Training timing

- **High-intensity sessions >3h before bed** — cortisol and core temp need time to clear.
- **Z1/Z2 evening sessions** are generally fine and may improve sleep onset via mild adenosine buildup and temp drop.

**Key reference:** Walker M (2017). *Why We Sleep.* Scribner. (Popular synthesis; primary sources cited within.)

## Sleep quantity targets

| Athlete load | Recommended sleep |
|---|---|
| CTL < 40 (moderate load) | 7.5–8.5h |
| CTL 40–70 (high load) | 8–9h |
| CTL > 70 / heavy training blocks | 9h+ with naps |
| Pre-race taper | Quality > quantity; sleep debt from taper restlessness is normal and not harmful |

**Note:** sleep extension (deliberately sleeping more during build phases) has been shown to improve reaction time, sprint performance, and mood in athletes. Even 30min extra per night over 5–6 weeks produces measurable gains.

**Key reference:** Mah CD et al. (2011). *The effects of sleep extension on the athletic performance of collegiate basketball players.* Sleep, 34(7), 943–950.

## Taper / race-week sleep

Athletes commonly sleep poorly the night before a race — this is normal and not performance-limiting if the preceding nights were adequate. The two nights before race day matter more than race-eve sleep.

- **Don't panic about race-eve sleep.** One bad night does not impair performance if you are not chronically sleep-deprived.
- **Extend sleep 2–3 nights before the race** — bank extra sleep in advance.
- **Avoid napping >20min on race day** — longer naps cause sleep inertia and may worsen race-eve sleep.

**Key reference:** Leeder J et al. (2012). *Sleep duration and quality in elite athletes measured using wristwatch actigraphy.* Journal of Sports Sciences, 30(6), 541–545.
