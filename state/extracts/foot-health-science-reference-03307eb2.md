## The Foot as an Active Structure

The foot is not a passive platform — it is an active, spring-like system that directly affects running efficiency and injury risk.

**Key structures:**
- **Medial longitudinal arch (MLA):** acts as a spring — stores and returns elastic energy during stance
- **Plantar fascia:** the passive tensile element; stiffens the arch via the windlass mechanism (toe extension → tension → arch locks)
- **Intrinsic foot muscles (IFM):** the active element — eccentrically control arch drop in early stance, concentrically stiffen the foot at push-off
- **Extrinsic muscles** (tibialis posterior, peroneals, FHL, FDL): control ankle and rearfoot, transmit force from calf complex

**Consequence for runners:** weak IFM → excessive arch deformation under load → energy leak at push-off → ↑ plantar fascia stress → ↑ navicular/metatarsal load → injury chain.

## Foot Orthoses and Insoles

### Do insoles prevent injuries?

**Yes — with moderate evidence.** Meta-analysis of 12 RCTs (Pires Neves 2022, n=5,321 runners):
- Injury rate: **37% control vs. 24% with orthoses**
- **Risk ratio = 0.60 (p<0.00001)** — **40% injury risk reduction**

This is a stronger finding than shoe-type selection (Cochrane, Relph 2022), which showed no benefit. The distinction: choosing a neutral vs. motion-control shoe does nothing; adding an insole that modifies foot mechanics does.

### Custom vs. off-the-shelf (OTS)

| Feature | Custom FOs | Off-the-shelf FOs |
|---|---|---|
| Plantar pressure ↓ at heel/forefoot | ✅ (SMD −0.35) | ✅ (SMD −1.03 at medial heel — often stronger) |
| Mid-foot contact ↑ (arch engaged) | ✅ | ✅ |
| Comfort improvement | ✅ significant | Moderate |
| Ankle inversion ↓ (with heel cup) | ✅ | Varies |
| Running economy effect | ⚠️ **slightly worse** (SMD −0.25) | Neutral |
| Cost | High (€150–400) | Low (€20–60) |

**Practical:** For healthy athletes without specific diagnosis, **off-the-shelf arch-support insoles** offer comparable or better pressure relief without the running economy cost of custom orthoses. Custom insoles are warranted for specific structural diagnoses (leg length discrepancy, marked pes planus/cavus with symptoms, post-surgical).

### What conditions benefit from insoles?

| Condition | Insole type | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **General injury prevention** | Arch-support (OTS) | Moderate — 40% injury risk ↓ |
| **Medial tibial stress syndrome (shin splints)** | Arch-support | Normalises pressure, reduces pain |
| **Patellofemoral pain (runner's knee)** | Medial-wedged | Reduces pain short-term |
| **Plantar fasciitis** | Arch-support + heel cushion | Strong — significantly improves symptoms |
| **Ankle instability (chronic)** | Textured insoles | Improves proprioception/balance |
| **Overpronation with symptoms** | Medial post + arch support | ↓ ankle inversion ROM |

### What insoles don't fix

- **Running economy** — custom FOs slightly worsen it; no insole enhances it
- **Biomechanical "correction" of healthy gait** — no evidence arch-type matching to shoe type prevents injury
- **Substituting for foot strengthening** — insoles support; they do not build strength; long-term passive support may actually reduce IFM stimulus

## Foot Strengthening

### Why it matters

Taddei et al. 2020 (RCT, 28 runners, 8 weeks):
- Foot strengthening → ↑ IFM volume → **↑ vertical propulsive impulse** at push-off
- Correlation: abductor hallucis and flexor digitorum brevis volume ~ propulsive force (r=0.40–0.69)
- **Foot strength is a running performance variable**, not just an injury-prevention metric

### Most effective exercises

| Exercise | Target | Difficulty | How to |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Short foot / arch doming** | All IFM; arch | Low | Sitting: shorten foot length by drawing heel toward metatarsals without curling toes; hold 5s × 10 reps |
| **Toe spread + press** | Intrinsic stabilisers | Low | Spread all toes, then press into floor independently |
| **Single-leg balance on unstable surface** | IFM + ankle stabilisers | Medium | 3 × 30–60s per side; progress to eyes closed |
| **Heel raises — single leg** | Extrinsic calf + toe flexors | Medium | 3 × 15–20; slow eccentric descent |
| **Towel scrunching** | Flexor digitorum brevis | Low | Seated: scrunch towel under foot with toes |
| **Hopping drills** | Dynamic IFM + tendon | High | Single-leg hops: 3 × 10–15; progress to multidirectional |

**Frequency:** 3–4 × /week. Gains appear within 4 weeks; plateau at 8–12 weeks if not progressed.

### Minimalist walking — the shortcut

Ridge et al. 2019 (RCT, 57 runners, 8 weeks): **minimalist shoe walking = dedicated foot exercises** for IFM strength and size gains. Both groups gained equally; control gained nothing.

**Protocol:** Walk 20–30 min/day in minimalist shoes (zero-drop, thin sole, flexible; e.g. Vivobarefoot, Xero); increase progressively each week. Keep all running in normal shoes during transition.

- Gains: strength +9–57%, muscle size +7–11% (Xu 2023 systematic review)
- No change needed to running training
- High compliance due to low effort

**Who benefits most:** athletes who sit at desks in rigid shoes all day — their IFM are chronically understimulated.

## Plantar Fasciitis (Plantar Fasciopathy)

**Prevalence:** ~30% of runners will experience plantar fasciitis at some point (Arnold & Moody 2018).

### Mechanism

Repetitive overload of the plantar fascia during stance phase, especially at the calcaneal insertion. Risk factors:
- Weak IFM (↑ fascia load)
- Tight calf / limited ankle dorsiflexion
- Sudden volume increase
- Hard surfaces + minimal cushioning
- High-arched (pes cavus) or low-arched (pes planus) feet

### Classic presentation

- **Morning first-step pain** (worst in first few steps, eases with walking) — hallmark
- Heel pain during/after running, worsening with volume
- Tender at medial calcaneal tubercle

### Treatment hierarchy (evidence-based)

| Step | Intervention | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Plantar fascia stretch** (pull toes into dorsiflexion, hold 30s × 3 before first step of day) | Strong |
| 1 | **Calf + Achilles stretching** (gastrocnemius + soleus) | Strong |
| 2 | **Eccentric heel drops** off a step (3 × 15 reps, twice daily) | Strong — tendon remodelling |
| 2 | **Arch-support insole** (OTS or custom) | Strong |
| 3 | **IFM strengthening** (short foot exercise, progressive loading) | Moderate |
| 3 | **Barefoot running on soft surface** (grass, 10–20 min progressive) | Preliminary — promising |
| 4 | **Shockwave therapy (ESWT)** | Moderate — effective for chronic (>3 months) cases |
| 5 | **Reduce running volume** 20–40% during acute phase | Clinical consensus |

**Do NOT stop running entirely** unless pain is >6/10 and worsening with each session. Relative load management + treatment is preferred over full rest.

**Return-to-full-training:** expect 6–12 weeks for acute PF; 3–6 months for chronic. Recurrence rate high if IFM weakness not addressed.

## Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome (MTSS / Shin Splints)

**Prevalence:** ~22% of runners (4th most common running injury).

**Mechanism:** Bending stress on the tibial cortex from repetitive loading; aggravated by navicular drop and excessive pronation.

**Key risk factors:** high navicular drop (arch flattening under load), hip internal rotation, high pelvic tilt, sudden volume increase.

**Treatment:**
- **Relative rest** (reduce volume 30–50%, avoid hard surfaces)
- **Arch-support orthoses** — normalise plantar pressure, ↓ pain (Menéndez 2020)
- **Hip/glute strengthening** — reduces internal rotation and pelvic drop
- **Gait retraining** (increase cadence, reduce stride length, land lighter)
- Shockwave therapy (ESWT) as adjunct (Rhim 2024)

## Most Common Running Injuries — Overview

| Injury | Prevalence | Primary cause | Key intervention |
|---|---|---|---|
| Iliotibial band syndrome | 34% | Hip abductor weakness, overstride | Glute med strengthening, cadence ↑ |
| **Plantar fasciitis** | 30% | IFM weakness, calf tightness, volume spike | Stretching, eccentric loading, insoles |
| Hamstring strain | 25% | Fatigue, speed, poor warm-up | Eccentric loading, progressive speed work |
| **MTSS (shin splints)** | 22% | Pronation, volume spike | Load management, orthoses, hip strength |
| Patellofemoral pain | Variable | Quad weakness, overpronation | Quad strengthening, medial-wedge insole |
| Achilles tendinopathy | Variable | Calf loading, sudden mileage ↑ | Eccentric heel drops, load management |

## Practical Decision Guide

```
"Do I need insoles?"
│
├── Symptomatic (PF, MTSS, PFP, shin pain) → YES — OTS arch-support first
├── High injury history / recurring lower limb problems → YES — try OTS
├── Currently healthy, no symptoms → foot strengthening is better investment
└── Custom orthoses? → only if OTS insufficient after 6–8 weeks + specific diagnosis

"Should I do foot strengthening?"
│
├── Running >30 km/week → YES — preventive
├── Wear dress shoes / rigid footwear all day → YES — IFM understimulated
├── History of PF, MTSS, navicular issues → YES — essential
└── Easiest route → minimalist shoe walking 20–30 min/day

"Signs I need to act on foot health NOW"
├── Morning first-step heel pain → plantar fasciitis protocol immediately
├── Shin pain on palpation of medial tibial border → MTSS protocol, reduce load
├── Navicular or midfoot pain after switching to carbon shoes → stop carbon shoes, consult
└── Ankle 'gives way' repeatedly → textured insoles + proprioception training
```
