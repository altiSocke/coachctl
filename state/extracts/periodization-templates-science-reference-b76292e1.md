# Periodization Templates — Science Reference

> This page covers general, athlete-agnostic frameworks. Event-specific personal targets (goal CTL, target TSB, race week plan) live in the personal repo's `profile/goals.md`.
> Key sources: Coggan (TrainingPeaks — CTL/ATL/TSB framework), Friel J. (*The Cyclist's Training Bible* 5th ed.; *The Triathlete's Training Bible* 5th ed.; TrainingPeaks blog — "Applying the Numbers" series 2015–2016), Schell D. (TrainingPeaks — "How to Plan Your Season with TSS", 2017), TrainingPeaks event guides (Marathon, Ultramarathon, Century 2022), Fitzgerald M. (*80/20 Running*, 2014), Seiler S. (multiple papers on polarized training, 2004–2013), Daniels J. (*Running Formula*, 3rd ed., 2014), Helgerud et al. (2007 — team sport VO₂max).
>
> **Confidence note on TSS tables:** Weekly TSS ranges are synthesized from Friel's CTL formulas, TrainingPeaks coaching documentation, and the mathematical framework (CTL × 7 ≈ weekly TSS). The underlying source charts (Schell 2017) are image-based and not directly machine-readable. These are evidence-informed ranges, not values from a controlled trial. Use them as starting points and calibrate against personal performance history.

## Event Demand Profiles

Understanding what each event requires physiologically determines how the plan is structured — how long each phase lasts, how high CTL must climb, and what proportion of training is low vs. high intensity.

| Event | Duration (trained amateur) | Primary limiter | Intensity character |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sprint triathlon** | ~1:00–1:20 | VO₂max, anaerobic capacity | Near-maximal throughout; Z4–Z5 dominant |
| **Half marathon** | ~1:10–2:30 | LT2 / VO₂max, running economy | Near or above LT2; high-quality work critical |
| **Olympic triathlon** | ~2:00–2:30 | Multi-sport aerobic capacity; brick adaptation | Bike ~90% FTP; run near 10k effort |
| **Marathon** | ~2:30–5:00 | Glycogen economy; LT1–LT2 corridor; musculoskeletal resilience | 82–87% of LT2 pace; volume-driven |
| **Gran fondo 200km+ (alpine)** | ~6–12 hours | Fat oxidation; pacing across repeated climbs; power-to-weight (W/kg) | Z2 base with sustained Z3–Z4 on climbs |
| **Ultra trail 50k** | ~5–10 hours | Fat oxidation; terrain adaptation; neuromuscular durability | Predominantly below LT1; hike/run alternation |
| **Ultra trail 100k** | ~10–30+ hours | All of 50k + GI resilience; heat/altitude/sleep tolerance | Near-exclusive sub-LT1 |

**Key axis:** The longer the event, the more volume dominates and the less absolute intensity matters. The shorter the event, the more top-end aerobic capacity (LT2, VO₂max) determines outcome. This axis governs every decision in the phase templates below.

## Phase Structure by Event Type

### Canonical Phase Model (Friel/TrainingPeaks)

Friel's macro-periodization model divides a training year into:

| Phase | Purpose | Typical duration |
|---|---|---|
| Transition / off-season | Recovery; unstructured activity | 2–4 weeks |
| Prep | Movement quality; aerobic base; form drills | 3–4 weeks |
| Base 1–3 | Aerobic endurance; force development; volume foundation | 9–12 weeks (3 × 3–4 week blocks) |
| Build 1–2 | Event-specific fitness; threshold and VO₂max development | 6–8 weeks (2 × 3–4 week blocks) |
| Peak | Race-intensity sharpening; volume reduction; intensity maintained | 2 weeks |
| Race / Taper | Final freshening; TSB positive | 1–2 weeks |

This yields a natural **24-week plan**. For a **16-week plan**, the Prep and Base phases collapse — Base is compressed to 6–7 weeks and assumes the athlete enters with an existing aerobic base (CTL ≥40–50). Taper duration (2–3 weeks) is fixed regardless of plan length.

### Event-Specific Phase Timelines

**Marathon**

| Phase | 16-week | 24-week | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Base | Wk 1–8 | Wk 1–12 | Z2 runs; weekly long run building to 30–35 km |
| Build | Wk 9–13 | Wk 13–19 | Tempo intervals; LT2 work; race-pace inserts in long runs |
| Peak | Wk 14–15 | Wk 20–21 | Race-simulation; reduce volume 20%; maintain quality |
| Taper | Wk 16 | Wk 22–24 | Volume –40–50%; 1 quality session/week; TSB rising |

Peak training long run: 32–34 km (not 42 km — muscle damage from race-distance runs exceeds recovery benefit). Last long run ≥3 weeks before race.

**Half Marathon**

| Phase | 12-week | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Base | Wk 1–5 | Z2 runs; build weekly volume; strides from week 2 |
| Build | Wk 6–10 | Threshold intervals (T-pace); VO₂max intervals (I-pace); long runs with HM-pace inserts |
| Taper | Wk 11–12 | Volume –40–50%; 1–2 quality sessions; tune-up race week 11 optional |

**Gran Fondo / 200km Alpine Sportive (cycling)**

| Phase | 16–20 weeks | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Base | Wk 1–6 | Z2 rides building to 3–5 hour endurance rides; high-cadence sessions |
| Build | Wk 7–14 | Sweet-spot intervals; FTP blocks; climbing intervals (20–40 min sustained climbs); 1 long ride/week extending to 5–7h |
| Peak | Wk 15–16 | Volume –30%; 1–2 intensity sessions; full-distance simulation ride if event >6h |
| Taper | Wk 17–20 | Volume –50%; spin legs; TSB target +15 to +20 on event day |

Training camp week (often week 10–14): 4–5 days of back-to-back long rides in alpine terrain. TSS 700–1100 for the week. Plan for 5–7 days recovery after before resuming normal build.

**Ultra Trail 50k**

| Phase | 20-week | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Base | Wk 1–8 | Z1–Z2 trail running; build to 15–18 hours/week at peak base; vertical gain accumulation |
| Build | Wk 9–16 | Hill intervals; back-to-back long runs (Sat 4–5h + Sun 2–3h); race-terrain simulation |
| Peak | Wk 17–18 | Volume –30%; 1 back-to-back weekend at reduced duration |
| Taper | Wk 19–20 | Volume –50%; no long runs >2h in final 10 days |

**Ultra Trail 100k**

Minimum preparation: 6–12 months from a solid aerobic base. Phase structure same as 50k but extended:
- Phase 1 (months 1–6): Volume-first; almost exclusively Z1–Z2; build to 20h+/week
- Phase 2 (months 7–9): Add intensity; hill intervals; back-to-back long runs increasing to 30–35h/week cumulative
- Phase 3 (4–8 weeks before event): Race-specificity (night running, altitude, nutrition testing)
- Taper (2–3 weeks): Volume –50%; no long runs >3h in final 2 weeks

**Olympic Triathlon**

| Phase | 17-week | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Base | Wk 1–6 | Aerobic work in all 3 sports; swim technique priority; no brick sessions |
| Build | Wk 7–14 | Bike FTP intervals; run threshold work; weekly brick sessions (bike→run); introduce swim speed |
| Peak | Wk 15–16 | Race-simulation brick (full or 80% distance); volume –25% |
| Taper | Wk 17 | Volume –50%; short intensity bursts; rest |

- Running: ~65 TSS/h (average IF ~0.83)

## Peak CTL Targets by Event and Athlete Level

These represent the CTL level arriving at the start of the taper (~2–3 weeks before race day). CTL will drop ~2–5 points during taper — the race-day CTL will be slightly lower.

> CTL is not comparable across sports: running CTL represents higher physiological cost than cycling CTL at the same numerical value. The values below are per-sport CTL for single-sport athletes.

| Event | Beginner / novice | Trained amateur | Advanced amateur |
|---|---|---|---|
| Half marathon | 40–55 | 55–75 | 75–95 |
| Marathon | 55–70 | 70–90 | 90–110 |
| Gran fondo 200km alpine | 65–85 | 85–110 | 110–140 |
| Ultra 50k (trail) | 60–75 | 75–95 | 95–120 |
| Ultra 100k (trail) | 75–95 | 95–120 | 120–150+ |
| Olympic tri (combined CTL) | 65–80 | 80–110 | 110–140 |

**Important qualifications:**

1. **CTL is relative, not absolute.** The most reliable target is "the CTL at which you've performed best historically." Industry ranges are starting points — if your best marathon happened at CTL 65, chasing CTL 90 may not improve the result.
2. **Ramp rate trumps absolute CTL.** Arriving at CTL 80 via a sustainable 5-point/week ramp produces a better-adapted athlete than arriving at CTL 85 via an 11-point/week ramp. The latter carries injury and overtraining risk (see `wiki/injury_prevention.md`).
3. **Running CTL burns hotter.** When combining run and bike, running TSS represents higher physiological cost for the same numerical value. Running-dominant athletes building toward a marathon need more recovery than cycling-dominant athletes at the same combined CTL.

### TSB on Race Day

Per Friel (TrainingPeaks "Applying the Numbers Part 3"):

| TSB | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| +15 to +25 | Target zone for A races — fresh and fit |
| +5 to +15 | B race window — good but not fully peaked |
| 0 to +5 | C race / training race |
| −5 to −15 | Normal training fatigue — do not race important events here |
| < −15 | Too fatigued — prioritise recovery |
| > +30 | Fitness decaying — taper was too long or too deep |

## Session Type Mix by Phase

Seiler's polarized training model and Fitzgerald's 80/20 framework both converge on the same core principle: 80% of training time below LT1 (Z1–Z2), 20% above LT2 (Z4–Z5), with minimal time in the grey zone (Z3, LT1–LT2).

### Phase-by-Phase Distribution (approximate)

| Phase | Z1–Z2 | Z3 (tempo) | Z4–Z5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Base** | 85–95% | ≤5% | 0–5% |
| **Build** | 70–80% | 5–10% | 15–20% |
| **Peak** | 60–70% | 5–10% | 20–25% |
| **Taper** | 65–75% | 5–10% | 15–20% (short; volume cut) |

### Event-Type Polarization Ratio at Peak Phase

Longer events require more volume dominance; shorter events reward top-end aerobic development.

| Event | % Z1–Z2 (peak) | % Z4–Z5 (peak) |
|---|---|---|
| Ultra trail 100k | 90–95% | 5–10% |
| Gran fondo 200km | 80–85% | 10–15% |
| Marathon | 75–80% | 15–20% |
| Half marathon | 70–75% | 20–25% |
| Olympic triathlon | 65–75% | 20–25% |
| Sprint triathlon | 60–70% | 25–30% |

**The grey zone trap** applies in every phase: athletes naturally drift to Z3 because it "feels hard enough." Z3 fatigues without producing the metabolic adaptations of Z2 or the neuromuscular quality of Z4–Z5. Keep Z3 deliberately limited; go easy on easy days and hard on hard days.

## Workout Archetypes

### Running

| Archetype | Phase | Zone | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Easy / base run** | Base–Race | Z1–Z2 | Conversational pace; 40–90 min; aerobic foundation |
| **Long slow run (LSR)** | Base–Build | Z1–Z2 | Primary volume driver; 90–210+ min; marathon: builds to 32–34 km |
| **Strides** | All phases | Z5+ (short) | 8–10 × 20 sec controlled accelerations after any easy run; neuromuscular |
| **Tempo / threshold** | Build–Peak | Z3–Z4 | 20–40 min at LT2 pace (Daniels' T-pace); critical for HM and marathon |
| **Lactate intervals** | Build–Peak | Z4 | 4–5 × 1200 m or 3–5 min at HM–10k effort; 1–2 min recovery |
| **VO₂max intervals** | Build–Peak | Z5 | 5 × 1000 m or 3–5 min at 5k–3k effort; Daniels' I-pace; key for HM |
| **Race-pace inserts** | Peak | Z3–Z4 | Marathon-pace miles in the second half of a long run |
| **Back-to-back long runs** | Build (ultra only) | Z1–Z2 | Sat 3–5h + Sun 2–3h; simulates ultra cumulative fatigue |
| **Hill repeats** | Build | Z4–Z5 | 8–15 × 30–60 sec at 6–10% grade; or 5–8 × 3–5 min; develops power and economy |
| **Progressive run** | Build | Z2→Z3 | First half easy; second half at marathon pace or better; builds fatigue resistance |

### Cycling

| Archetype | Phase | Zone | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Endurance ride** | Base–Race | Z2 | Long aerobic ride; gran fondo: build to 5–7h; key volume driver |
| **High-cadence spin** | Base | Z2 | 90–100 rpm; 60–120 min; neuromuscular efficiency development |
| **Sweet spot / tempo intervals** | Build | Z3–Z4 | 2–3 × 20 min at 88–93% FTP; high TSS efficiency per training hour |
| **FTP / threshold intervals** | Build–Peak | Z4 | 3–4 × 8–12 min at 95–105% FTP; 5 min recovery |
| **VO₂max intervals** | Build–Peak | Z5 | 5–8 × 3–5 min at 110–120% FTP; equal rest |
| **Climbing intervals** | Build (gran fondo) | Z3–Z4 | 3–5 × 15–30 min sustained climbs at LT; specific for alpine events |
| **Race-simulation ride** | Peak | Mixed | Full-distance or 80% distance at target effort; tests nutrition and pacing |

### Triathlon-Specific

| Archetype | Phase | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Brick (bike → run)** | Build–Peak | Immediate run off the bike; builds neuromuscular and metabolic adaptation for the T2 transition |
| **Race-simulation brick** | Peak | Full or 80% distance; dress rehearsal 2–3 weeks out |
| **Open-water swim practice** | Build | Technique in race conditions; sighting, drafting, wetsuit use |
| **Interval swim** | Build–Peak | 10–20 × 100m with rest; or 4–6 × 400m with 30 sec rest; builds swim economy |

## Concurrent Run + Bike Periodization

When running and cycling are both part of the training mix, the following rules reduce overtraining and ensure each discipline is peaking at the right time.

### Rule 1: Avoid parallel peaks

If the A-race is a **marathon**, reduce cycling to 2–3 easy rides/week (maintenance) for the final 8 weeks of the run build. If the A-race is a **gran fondo**, reduce running to 2–3 easy runs/week for the final 8 weeks of the bike build. Trying to peak both simultaneously produces mediocre results in both.

### Rule 2: TSS budget by phase

A trained amateur managing 600–800 combined TSS/week should allocate:

| Season focus | Bike TSS % | Run TSS % |
|---|---|---|
| Bike-dominant (pre-gran fondo) | 70–80% | 20–30% |
| Run-dominant (pre-marathon) | 20–30% | 70–80% |
| General maintenance / cross-training | 50% | 50% |

### Rule 3: Cross-training credit

Easy cycling provides aerobic maintenance at low musculoskeletal cost. During a heavy run build, replacing one Z2 easy run per week with a Z2 bike ride reduces injury risk without sacrificing CTL. This is particularly useful when approaching high weekly run volumes or recovering from minor soft tissue soreness.

### Rule 4: Combined ramp rate

Regardless of sport mix, do not increase **combined weekly TSS** by more than 8–10% for more than 3 consecutive weeks. A recovery week at 50–60% of peak combined TSS is required after every 3-week build block.

### Rule 5: Running TSS weighing

Running TSS is physiologically more costly than cycling TSS at the same numerical value (eccentric loading, higher cardiovascular demand per equivalent pace/power). When combined on a single Performance Management Chart, some coaches apply a 1.2–1.5× multiplier to running TSS to account for this. This is a practical heuristic, not a published formula — use consistently if adopted.

## Team Sport Season Periodization (Unihockey / Floorball)

Team sports follow a fundamentally different periodization structure from single-event endurance. The Coggan CTL/TSS framework is not natively designed for team sports; Foster's **session RPE method** (sRPE = RPE × duration in minutes) is more commonly used for training load monitoring.

| Season phase | Duration | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Off-season conditioning** | 8–12 weeks | Aerobic base (running/cycling); strength foundation; VO₂max intervals |
| **Pre-season** | 4–6 weeks | On-ice/court-specific conditioning; scrimmages; reduce pure endurance volume |
| **Early in-season** | 4–8 weeks | Match readiness; 1–2 aerobic sessions/week off-ice; taper into first competitive block |
| **Mid-season maintenance** | Season length | 1–2 off-ice aerobic sessions/week; strength maintenance 1–2×/week |
| **Playoff peak** | 2–4 weeks before playoffs | Taper; reduce total volume; maintain intensity |
| **Transition / recovery** | 2–4 weeks | Unstructured; active recovery; strength deload |

**Aerobic target for team sport:** VO₂max of 55–65 ml/kg/min correlates with top-tier amateur unihockey performance (Helgerud et al. 2007, evidence derived from comparable intermittent field sports). High-intensity interval training (4 × 4 min at 90–95% HRmax, 3 min active recovery, 2×/week) is the most efficient method to develop VO₂max for team sport athletes in the off-season conditioning phase.
