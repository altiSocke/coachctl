# Stretching — Science Reference

Synthesised from: `raw/science/stretching_endurance_team_sports.md` (2026-04-25)  
Primary sources: Warneke et al. 2025 (meta-analysis, PMID 40442558), Panascì et al. 2024 (RCT, PMID 39608347), Faelli et al. 2021, Ullman et al. 2021, Kurt et al. 2023, Parr et al. 2025, Park et al. 2018.

---

## The Core Finding

The long-held belief that pre-exercise static stretching impairs endurance performance by reducing tendon stiffness is **not supported by current evidence** at normal warm-up durations.

> *"Current evidence does not support any effect of stretching on running economy acutely — for static, dynamic, OR PNF stretching."*  
> — Warneke et al., Sports Med Open, 2025 (15 studies, n=181, PRISMA meta-analysis)

This reverses ~20 years of "don't stretch before running" dogma. The practical implication: athletes should not avoid stretching to "protect stiffness."

---

## Types of Stretching

| Type | Definition | Timing | Best use |
|------|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Static (SS)** | Hold at end-range, no movement | Post-exercise or standalone | ROM gains, injury prevention, recovery |
| **Dynamic (DS)** | Controlled movement through full range (leg swings, hip circles, walking lunges) | Pre-exercise | Warm-up, neural priming, RE improvement |
| **PNF** | Contract-relax-stretch cycles; requires partner or anchor | Post-exercise or standalone | Maximum ROM gains; adductor injury prevention |
| **Ballistic** | Bouncing at end-range | Rarely recommended | High injury risk; avoid unless sport-specific |
| **Foam rolling (SMR)** | Self-myofascial release | Pre or post | Complements stretching; improves ROM and recovery; does not replace stretching |

---

## The Duration Rule

Duration of holds is the critical variable — more important than stretch type:

| Hold duration | Effect |
|---|---|
| ≤30s per muscle | Safe and beneficial — no force/stiffness loss |
| 30–60s per muscle (DS) | Beneficial: ↑ RE, ↑ time-to-exhaustion (Panascì 2024) |
| 60s per muscle (SS) | Neutral — no harm but also less acute benefit than DS |
| >60s per muscle (SS), immediately pre-exercise | Possible transient stiffness/force loss — avoid before sprints/intensity |
| 2–3 min per muscle, standalone session | Maximum long-term ROM gains |

## Pre-Exercise Stretching (Warm-Up)

### What the evidence says

1. **Dynamic stretching (DS) is the preferred pre-exercise modality** — it improves running economy and time-to-exhaustion at moderate durations (60s/leg) (Panascì 2024 RCT)
2. **Short static stretching (≤30s) is neutral to beneficial** — does not impair RE or force output; reduces RPE (Faelli 2021)
3. **Long static stretching (>60s), immediately before explosive efforts** — may transiently reduce maximal force; avoid before sprints, jumps, or game starts
4. **Isometric warm-up drills** (wall sits, isometric calf holds) preserve force output better than SS before explosive sport (Ullman 2021) — relevant for hockey/unihockey

### Warm-up sequence (universal)

**Always warm tissue first — never stretch cold muscle.**

```
Step 1 (5–10 min): aerobic activation — easy jog, light spin, skip
Step 2 (8–12 min): dynamic stretching — sport-specific movements
Step 3 (optional, ≤30s holds): short static holds for areas of acute tightness
Step 4: sport-specific activation (strides, accelerations, drills)
```

## Post-Exercise Stretching (Cool-Down)

Post-exercise is where **long-term ROM gains and injury prevention** happen.

- Muscle is warm → more compliant → larger ROM gains from same stretch
- No performance cost (training is finished)
- 2 sets × 30–60s per muscle group
- PNF (contract-relax) produces the fastest ROM gains — use 3× per week for deficit areas

**Minimum effective post-session routine (10–15 min):**

| Muscle | Stretch | Duration |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Hip flexors (psoas/rectus femoris) | Deep lunge, front knee at 90°, back knee grounded | 60s/side |
| Hamstrings | Standing forward fold or supine strap | 60s/side |
| Glutes/piriformis | Figure-4 (supine or seated) | 60s/side |
| Calves (gastrocnemius) | Straight-leg wall stretch | 60s/side |
| Calves (soleus) | Bent-knee wall stretch | 60s/side |
| Adductors | Butterfly or sumo squat hold | 60s |
| Thoracic spine | Foam roller extension (see cycling section) | 90s |
| Neck/levator scapulae | Ear-to-shoulder, chin-to-chest | 30s each |

---

## Standalone Flexibility Sessions (Off-Day)

3× per week, 20–30 min. This is the highest ROI stretching investment:
- Chronic ROM gains accumulate over 4–8 weeks
- Directly enables better bike position, reduced injury risk, improved movement patterns
- Use PNF for areas with largest deficits (typically hip flexors, hamstrings, thoracic in cyclists; adductors in team sports athletes)

## Injury Prevention: What the Evidence Actually Supports

Stretching alone has **weak direct injury prevention evidence**. What works in combination:

| Intervention | Evidence | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Dynamic warm-up (replaces static-only) | Strong (FIFA 11+, analogues) | ↓ lower limb injuries ~30–50% |
| Eccentric strengthening (Nordic curls, Copenhagen adductor) | Very strong | ↓ hamstring/adductor injury rates |
| **Stretching + strength combined** | Moderate | Better than either alone |
| Chronic stretching (3×/week, 4–8 weeks) | Moderate | ↑ ROM → reduced mechanical load at end-range → injury reduction |
| Foam rolling alone | Weak | ↑ ROM acutely; limited injury prevention evidence alone |

Triathlete survey (Parr 2025, n=219): among injury-free athletes, 72.2% cited strength training, 64.9% cited stretching/foam rolling as key prevention strategies.

**Bottom line:** regular stretching is part of the injury prevention package — but it must be combined with eccentric strength work, not used as the sole intervention.

## Sport-Specific Protocols

### Running

**Pre-run (total ~10 min after 5–10 min easy jog):**
- Leg swings front-back: 15×/side
- Leg swings lateral: 15×/side
- Walking lunges: 10× each leg
- Hip circles: 10×/side
- Ankle circles + heel raises: 15×/side
- Optional: ≤30s hold per tight area (calf, hip flexor)

**Post-run (10–15 min):**
- Calf (gastrocnemius + soleus), hamstring, hip flexor (lunge), glute (figure-4), ITB/TFL (standing cross-leg)
- 60s × 2 per muscle group

**Chronic deficit priority:** most runners are tight in hip flexors and calves — address these 3× per week off-day with PNF or 2–3 min holds.

### Cycling

**Pre-ride (5 min):**
- Hip circles, leg swings, thoracic rotations, ankle circles — dynamic only
- Static stretching pre-ride is lower priority than post-ride for cyclists

**Post-ride (15 min) — HIGH PRIORITY:**
Hip flexors, hamstrings, adductors (butterfly), thoracic extension (foam roller), glutes, calves, neck.  
The fixed flexed-hip position of cycling chronically shortens hip flexors, restricts thoracic extension, and loads the lumbar spine. Post-ride stretching is more critical for cyclists than for runners.

**Bike position link:**
- **Tight hip flexors** → anterior pelvic tilt → impaired low back stability → limits aero position depth and duration
- **Tight hamstrings** → reduced posterior pelvic rotation → limits reach to drops/aero bars; alters dead-spot at BDC
- **Restricted thoracic extension** → inability to hold aggressive fit → neck compensates → cervical pain; increases aerodynamic drag penalty
- **Chronic thoracic + hip flexor stretching** is a prerequisite for athletes pursuing aggressive/aero bike fits

**Thoracic extension on foam roller:** lie transversely over roller at mid-thoracic level, arms crossed over chest, extend gently over the roller for 90s. Move roller 2–3 positions along the spine. This is the single highest-ROI flexibility intervention for road/gravel cyclists.

**Off-day (3× per week):**
Yoga-style: pigeon pose (hip external rotation + hip flexor), lizard lunge (deep hip flexor), seated forward fold (hamstrings), thoracic rotation drills. 20–30 min.

### Unihockey / Hockey / Stop-and-Go Indoor Sports

Stop-and-go sports demand explosive lateral movement, rapid direction changes, and sprint starts — injury profile is distinctly different from pure endurance sports.

**Dominant injury sites:**
- Adductor/groin strains — explosive lateral loading and cutting
- Hamstring strains — sprint starts from static
- Ankle sprains — dorsiflexion restriction
- Patellar tendinopathy — repeated jump-landing
- Lumbar load — low athletic stance maintained between plays

**Pre-game/practice (10–15 min after light aerobic activation):**
- Lateral leg swings: 15×/side
- Hip circles: 10×/side
- Cossack squat (deep lateral lunge): 8×/side
- Reverse lunges with thoracic rotation: 10×/side
- Sumo squat hold + lateral rock: 10× continuous
- Jumping jacks + skipping: 2 min
- Short acceleration runs (50–75%): 4×20m
- **Avoid long static holds** — explosive power needed immediately

**Post-game/practice (10–15 min):**
- Adductor butterfly: 60s
- Hip flexor (kneeling lunge): 60s/side
- Hamstring (supine): 60s/side
- Glute/piriformis (figure-4): 60s/side
- Hip internal rotation (seated figure-4 reverse): 60s/side
- Calf (gastro + soleus): 60s/side

**Injury prevention (3×/week off-session):**
- Copenhagen adductor programme (eccentric): 3 sets — primary evidence-based intervention for groin injury
- Nordic curl (eccentric hamstring): 3 sets
- PNF adductor stretch: contract-relax × 3 at end-range
- Single-leg calf raises (eccentric): 3×15 — Achilles/ankle protection

**Why adductor stretching alone is insufficient:**
Static adductor stretching increases ROM but does not build the eccentric strength needed to decelerate the adductors during explosive lateral movements. The Copenhagen Adductor Programme addresses the strength component — stretching supports it but cannot replace it.

## Key Numbers at a Glance

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Pre-exercise SS hold | ≤30s per muscle group |
| Pre-exercise DS duration | 60s per leg/muscle group (optimal per Panascì 2024) |
| Post-exercise SS hold | 30–60s × 2 sets per muscle group |
| PNF hold (contract phase) | 6–10s contraction, then relax and deepen |
| Standalone session frequency | 3×/week off-day |
| Weeks to meaningful ROM gain | 4–8 weeks with consistent practice |
| Injury prevention onset | 6+ weeks of consistent dynamic warm-up + eccentric strength |

## Evidence Limitations

- Most stretching RCTs have n=8–15 — effect sizes have wide confidence intervals
- Chronic stretching effects are understudied (Warneke 2025 explicitly flags this gap)
- Cycling-specific stretching RCTs are nearly absent
- Unihockey/floorball literature nonexistent on PubMed — evidence extrapolated from hockey, handball, soccer
- Individual variability is high: athletes with existing flexibility deficits gain more than already-flexible athletes
